# Research Report Writer

## Purpose

Turn research material into clear, rigorous, readable report prose. Borrow literary craft only for structure, rhythm, continuity, and concision; preserve the report's objective, evidence-led, professional tone.

## Core Workflow

1. Identify the report type, target reader, and decision use: investment memo, strategy report, fund research, macro note, industry analysis, policy brief, or business research.
2. State the core question before integrating source material. Ask: what is the phenomenon, what was the prior dominant driver, what has changed, and what decision does the reader need to make?
3. Extract the main thesis, supporting evidence, contradictions, recommended actions, and risks before writing.
4. Separate evidence by causal role and horizon: structural driver, cyclical driver, marginal pricing factor, catalyst, confirmation signal, or noise. Do not list factors as equals unless they truly have equal weight.
5. Pressure-test the source frame before adopting it. A recent event, repeated phrase, or vivid data point may be a trigger rather than the root cause.
6. Write the strongest professional objection to the planned thesis, then revise the thesis or scope conditions before drafting.
7. Rebuild the report around a visible line of reasoning. Prefer "question -> current state -> driver hierarchy -> evidence -> tension -> implication -> risk" over raw fact accumulation.
8. Draft from high-level judgment to supporting detail. Put the key conclusion early, then develop it step by step.
9. Rewrite paragraphs for flow: start with the core claim, add evidence, name the tension or limitation, then close with the analytical meaning.
10. Polish language for restraint, precision, and rhythm. Remove filler, duplicated claims, excessive modifiers, and undefined concept shifts.
11. End with a clean synthesis that returns to the opening issue and clarifies what should be watched next.

## Literary Craft Adapted For Research

Use these techniques as analytical tools, not decoration.

### Structure: Qi Cheng Zhuan He

Apply "opening, development, turn, close" at the report, section, and paragraph levels.

- Opening: state the central judgment or problem.
- Development: present evidence, data, or mechanism.
- Turn: introduce constraint, contradiction, valuation issue, timing issue, or uncertainty.
- Close: summarize the implication for decision-making.

### Rhythm: Long And Short Sentences

Use longer sentences for background, causal chains, and multi-factor explanations. Use short sentences for key judgments.

Avoid a page of long clauses. Avoid a pile of fragments. Let analytical prose breathe.

### Narrative Line: Restrained Nonfiction

Organize facts along a calm sequence such as:

- current state -> change -> cause -> impact -> next signal
- position -> space -> verification -> risk
- phenomenon -> mechanism -> pricing -> allocation implication

Do not invent drama. Make the causal line perceptible.

### Layering: From Surface To Mechanism

When analyzing a market, company, fund, policy, or industry, move from observable phenomenon to underlying driver, then to implication:

- What changed?
- Why did it change?
- Is the change cyclical, structural, or merely noisy?
- What has the market already priced?
- What evidence would confirm or falsify the view?

### Concision: Lian Zi Shan Fan

After drafting, remove words that do not change the meaning. Delete empty transitions such as "in fact", "as everyone knows", "to some extent", "it has to be said", and similar padding unless they serve a real logical function.

Keep key concepts consistent throughout the report. Do not rotate among synonyms if the distinction is not meaningful.

### Closure: Echo The Opening

Use the ending to return to the opening question. Close with the main judgment, the decision implication, and the next variables to track.

## Anti-Grab-Bag Discipline

Reports fail when they turn many true statements into one unfocused explanation. Before drafting, build a compact factor map:

| Factor | Horizon | Role | Direction | Weight | Evidence Strength | Priced In? |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|

Use it to decide what belongs in the thesis, what belongs in risk/watchpoints, and what should be cut.

### Mainline Before Materials

Always identify the mainline before summarizing the sources:

- What is the long-term or primary driver of the phenomenon?
- What is currently controlling marginal pricing or near-term behavior?
- Is the current move a regime change, a cyclical phase, a catalyst-driven repricing, or position/liquidity noise?
- Which evidence would prove the thesis wrong?

### Common Failure Modes

Avoid these patterns:

- Treating a fresh catalyst as the original cause of a long-running trend.
- Giving structural, cyclical, and tactical factors the same weight.
- Hiding uncertainty by harmonizing contradictory arguments into a safe middle ground.
- Writing consensus paragraphs that sound reasonable but add no decision value.
- Preserving a source's narrative frame when the frame itself needs to be challenged.

### Counterargument Pass

Before final output, write the strongest skeptical reader objection in one or two sentences. Revise the report if that objection exposes a confused driver, an overstated conclusion, or a missing scope condition.

## Report Patterns

### Market Or Strategy Report

Prefer this sequence:

1. Core judgment.
2. What the market previously treated as the dominant driver.
3. What is controlling marginal pricing now.
4. What is different from the previous comparable period.
5. Which structural, cyclical, and tactical factors have confirmation.
6. Which directions are near reversal or exhaustion.
7. Allocation implications.
8. Risks, falsification tests, and verification signals.

### Industry Or Company Chain Report

Prefer this sequence:

1. Demand beta and industry position.
2. Supply, inventory, capacity, and pricing cycle.
3. Profit distribution across the chain.
4. Valuation and crowding.
5. Key beneficiaries and weak links.
6. Watchpoints and risks.

### Fund Or Manager Research

Prefer this sequence:

1. Strategy positioning and role in portfolio.
2. Return source and market environment fit.
3. Risk exposure, drawdown behavior, and capacity.
4. Process, team, operational discipline, and repeatability.
5. Current suitability and monitoring indicators.

## Rewrite Standards

When rewriting existing material:

- Preserve factual meaning, numbers, dates, entities, and risk caveats.
- Do not add unsupported claims.
- Compress tables into the few conclusions they support unless the user asks for full detail.
- Convert bullet sprawl into paragraphs when a stronger line of reasoning is needed.
- Challenge the source's causal frame when it confuses a catalyst with the main driver.
- Keep headings informative. Prefer "AI Still Leads, But Selection Matters" over vague headings like "Analysis".
- If the original report has compliance disclaimers, do not rewrite them into investment advice.

## Language Rules

Write in a restrained research voice:

- Clear before elegant.
- Specific before abstract.
- Evidence before conclusion when the conclusion is non-obvious.
- Short conclusion after complex explanation.
- Neutral verbs over emotional verbs.
- "May", "could", "suggests", and "needs verification" when evidence is incomplete.

Prefer:

- "The signal is improving, but confirmation is still insufficient."
- "The opportunity lies less in broad beta and more in earnings verification."
- "The market is not simply trading risk appetite; it is repricing the durability of demand."

Avoid:

- lyrical metaphors
- poetic imagery
- exaggerated contrast
- emotional judgment
- promotional phrasing
- suspenseful setup
- decorative parallelism
- subjective admiration or disappointment

## Quality Checklist

Before responding, check:

- The first section states the central thesis clearly.
- The report names the prior dominant driver and the current marginal driver when analyzing a changing market or policy environment.
- Structural, cyclical, and tactical factors are separated rather than piled together.
- No fresh catalyst is allowed to masquerade as the root cause of a long-running trend.
- Each section advances the argument rather than repeating the summary.
- Data points are tied to analytical meaning.
- The strongest skeptical objection has been considered and either answered or reflected in scope conditions.
- The paragraph rhythm alternates explanation and judgment.
- The text has no empty throat-clearing phrases.
- Risks are present and not perfunctory.
- The conclusion returns to the opening question.

## Output Guidance

When the user asks for a trial rewrite, output the rewritten report directly. Do not create files unless requested.

When the source is long, produce a concise rewritten version that captures the thesis, structure, evidence, implications, and risks. State if the output is a selective rewrite rather than a full translation of every line.

When asked to create a formal document, use the relevant document skill or local file tools after drafting the report content.
